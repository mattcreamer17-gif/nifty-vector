A read on the stack

A read on where tracking, training, and shipping NiftoTrack actually split across AI, code, and human judgment.

Nifty Vector has a small, specific, well-scoped technical bet: submillimeter tracking in a form factor small enough to embed in a probe, a helmet, or a prop. The eMerge 2025 demo is clean. The patent filings in Canada and Australia are real. The ideaBRIDGE residency and the Invest Nova Scotia Accelerate placement are the right signals for a two-person Halifax deep-tech shop heading into a first commercial pilot. The harder work starts now: picking which vertical converts first, building the independent benchmark that a medical-device OEM will actually accept, and keeping the context clean across Nifty Vector, Scalespace, and Nummist Media as the contractor roster rotates.

Our working read: most of the next twelve months is plain code and documentation discipline. About thirty percent is rule-based logic for sensor fusion, calibration, and the integration contracts between NiftoTrack and whatever probe or helmet it drops into. The genuine AI work, where a model actually earns its place, is narrow: learned noise rejection, possibly learned calibration per device, and the content pipeline that turns raw tracker data into artifacts a buyer can evaluate. A 60/30/10 read on your own stack keeps the engineering cadence honest and the pitch deck specific.

Closest precedent

The closest precedent in our work is Feeld. Their CTO Andrew Santus ran a scoped sprint with us to build the organizational context architecture for a product team trying to operationalize AI internally. Workshop, advisory calls, a written Strategic Operations Framework the team could hand to new hires on day one. The deliverable was not a new product. It was a cleaner map of how the team already thought.

The methodology

Interpretable Context Methodology, published through ACM TiiS: github.com/RinDig/Interpretable-Context-Methodology-ICM-. Agent context as a layered filesystem, measured for interpretability and reproducibility. Read the paper, push back on the parts that do not hold up, and we can talk about whether a one-week application to your team would return the hours it costs.
